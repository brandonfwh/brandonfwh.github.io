TSA Gravity Car

Gravitational Potential Energy Vehicle // Pulley-Driven Propulsion // TSA State Competition

Overview

The TSA Gravity Car is a competition vehicle designed and built for the TSA engineering event. The challenge requires constructing a car whose wheels are driven exclusively by the gravitational potential energy of a standard Coke can. No motors, batteries, rubber bands, or other stored energy sources are allowed. The only propulsion comes from the falling motion of the can, which is attached to a string routed through a pulley system that converts vertical descent into horizontal wheel rotation.

Design & Engineering

The vehicle uses a pulley system that mechanically links the falling Coke can to the drive axle. As the can drops, the string unwinds and turns the wheels through the pulley's mechanical advantage. Key engineering decisions included optimizing the pulley ratio for the best balance between torque (to overcome initial friction and get the car rolling) and speed (to maximize distance traveled), minimizing rolling resistance through wheel selection and axle lubrication, and keeping overall mass as low as possible while maintaining structural rigidity.

The car placed 6th at the Georgia state TSA competition, competing against entries from schools across the state.

Medical Robot CAD & FEA

Autonomous AI-Powered Medical Robotics // 3-Axis Motion System // Startup Engineering

Overview

I was brought on to create the complete CAD model and perform finite element analysis for a small startup developing autonomous, AI-powered medical robots. The robot's mechanical platform uses a 3D-printer-like Cartesian axis motion system (X, Y, Z linear rails with stepper-driven lead screws) to achieve the precision positioning required for medical applications.

CAD & Analysis Work

The CAD model was built in Fusion 360 and encompasses the full mechanical assembly: the frame structure, all three axis carriages with linear rail mounts, stepper motor brackets, lead screw assemblies, and the end-effector mounting plate. The design needed to balance rigidity (to maintain positioning accuracy under load) with weight (for portability in clinical settings).

FEA simulations were run on critical structural components to validate that deflection under expected operating loads stayed within the tolerance required for medical precision. Stress concentrations at mounting points and rail interfaces were identified and addressed through design revisions before any physical prototyping began.

Orbital Mechanics Simulator

Newtonian Gravity Simulation // Self-Initiated AP Physics Project // Real-Time Visualization

Overview

Built on my own initiative for AP Physics, this orbital mechanics simulator models gravitational interactions between celestial bodies in real time. The simulation implements Newton's law of universal gravitation to compute attractive forces between all bodies, then numerically integrates their trajectories to produce realistic orbital paths, elliptical orbits, gravitational slingshots, and multi-body interactions.

The project demonstrates applied understanding of key physics concepts including gravitational force, centripetal acceleration, orbital velocity, conservation of energy and angular momentum, and the relationship between initial conditions and orbital shape (circular, elliptical, parabolic, and hyperbolic trajectories).

ESP32 Pip-Boy 3000

Wearable Embedded Computer // Multi-Phase Build // 15+ Integrated Components

Overview

The Pip-Boy 3000 is a fully functional wearable computer inspired by the Fallout franchise, but designed with a practical-first philosophy. Rather than building a cosplay prop, I set out to create a real embedded system that serves useful functions: live weather, compass heading, environmental readings, a stopwatch, ultrasonic radar, flashlight control, and more. It happens to look like a Pip-Boy, but every feature is genuinely useful.

The build was developed across multiple phases over several months, starting from getting pixels on a single display and progressively adding a second microcontroller, three screens, a full sensor suite, a LiPo battery system, and finally a custom 3D-printed enclosure.

Dual-MCU Architecture

The system uses two ESP32-WROOM-32E microcontrollers communicating over a UART serial link at 115200 baud using a JSON message protocol. The Main Controller (ESP32 #1) drives the primary 4-inch TFT touchscreen (Hosyond 480x320 ST7796S over SPI), manages the tab-based menu system, handles WiFi/Bluetooth connectivity, and runs the calendar, weather, and compass tabs. The Sensor Hub (ESP32 #2) drives the 0.96-inch SSD1306 OLED as a persistent status bar and the I2C LCD 1602 as a quick-glance readout, while managing all physical sensors: BME280 (temperature, humidity, pressure), ultrasonic distance, photoresistor, and the OV2640 camera.

The decision to use two microcontrollers instead of one was driven by GPIO limitations. With three displays (SPI + two I2C), a camera, six sensors, an LED strip, audio output, and WiFi all competing for pins, a single ESP32 could not handle the load. Splitting responsibilities between two MCUs simplified wiring and let each processor focus on its role.

Display System

Each of the three displays serves a distinct role. The 4-inch TFT is the primary interface, rendering a tab bar across the top and full-screen content for whichever of the 10 tabs is selected. I wrote the display driver using the TFT_eSPI library with custom PWM backlight control, which required resolving a conflict between the backlight PWM channel and the NeoPixel LED strip that was causing brightness flickering on both.

The OLED acts as a persistent status bar showing time, battery percentage, and WiFi/Bluetooth connection icons. The LCD 1602 provides a quick-glance readout of the current mode name and a single key value (like "TEMP: 72.4F") for at-a-glance information without needing to look at the main screen.

Power System & Enclosure

The device runs on dual 18650 LiPo cells managed by a TP4056 charging module with a 5V buck converter for stable regulation. A voltage divider feeds into an ADC pin for real-time battery monitoring displayed on the OLED status bar. The enclosure was designed in Fusion 360 and printed on a Bambu Lab P1S in PETG. The shell mounts the TFT screen on the top face with all electronics housed parallel on the underside, strapping to the forearm with adjustable Velcro bands.

Sediment Sentry Buoy

Autonomous Turbidity Monitoring Device // 4-Iteration Engineering Process // ~$35 Build Cost

Overview

The Sediment Sentry is an autonomous, GPS-tagged turbidity monitoring buoy designed to transform water quality measurement from a labor-intensive single-point process into a continuous, spatially-aware data stream. A researcher studying sediment transport traditionally must visit each site, submerge a handheld meter, and record one reading at a time. The Sediment Sentry captures continuous data at 60-second intervals, revealing dynamic patterns like rainfall-driven turbidity spikes and sediment plume migration that snapshot measurements would miss entirely.

Iterative Engineering Process

The development of the Sediment Sentry was conducted over four distinct engineering iterations, moving from a raw electronic proof-of-concept to a fully autonomous, field-ready scientific tool.

Iteration 1: Breadboard Prototyping and Logic Definition
The first phase focused entirely on proving the core scientific concept: measuring turbidity using electronics. The initial prototype consisted of sensor calibration on a breadboard. To establish a baseline, five turbidity standards were prepared using bentonite clay, and ten trials were conducted per sample to record data. During this testing, a highly variable response and severe ambient light issues were discovered. To handle the variability, the Arduino code was changed to categorize the water clarity into three distinct logical states: CLEAN, WARNING, and DANGER. This established the foundation for the data logging requirements.

Iteration 2: Power, Logging, and Calibration
With the core logic established, the focus shifted to making the device capable of remote data collection. This phase introduced SD card logging and battery integration. Short testing simulations were run, which led to the key discovery of a seemingly faulty sensor. To stabilize the readings, a polynomial calibration curve (with an R-squared of 0.987) was developed and programmed directly into the Arduino sketch. Using the new SD card module, the system was successfully programmed to log individual WARNING or DANGER areas alongside precise timestamps.

Iteration 3: Geolocation and Autonomy
A turbidity sensor is only useful if researchers know where the pollution is located. Iteration 3 transformed the project into a spatial mapping tool. An Arduino GPS module was integrated into the circuit, and the Arduino sketch was rewritten to handle autonomous 60-second sampling with CSV output. Rigorous system checks were performed, including an 11.7-hour battery duration test and GPS accuracy tests across 5 different campus locations. The micro SD card was successfully configured to record the time elapsed, the turbidity/clarity of the water, and the exact location of each entry. This data allowed the device to mark the geographical location for the most polluted areas, successfully turning it into a tool capable of finding a pollution hotspot or source.

Iteration 4: Waterproofing and Field Deployment
The final iteration addressed the physical constraints of deploying sensitive electronics in a harsh aquatic environment. With the sensor and code working perfectly, the final requirement was waterproofing the Overture PLA Air case. The physical durability of the design was put to the test through a 6-hour creek field deployment during a light rain, as well as a 3x deploy-retrieve durability test. After researching several methods like clear coating, the final design utilized a layer of clear epoxy to permanently seal all openings, including the switch, the sensor, and the lid.

Key Results

During field testing, the Sediment Sentry captured a rainfall-driven turbidity spike from 14 NTU to 187 NTU and its full two-hour decay curve. A single manual reading would have missed this dynamic event entirely. The GPS-tagged CSV data enabled spatial turbidity mapping, turning a waterway into a living dataset.

Sound Alert Pro

AI-Powered Sound Monitoring System // YAMNet (521 Categories) // Real-Time Classification

Overview

Sound Alert Pro is a real-time sound monitoring and classification system running on a Raspberry Pi. It uses Google's YAMNet model (a TensorFlow Lite audio classifier trained on AudioSet) to identify over 521 distinct sound categories from live microphone input, then triggers configurable alerts when specific sounds are detected above a confidence threshold. The system was designed for home safety applications: detecting fire alarms, glass breaking, sirens, doorbells, and other critical sounds, then pushing instant notifications to the user's phone.

How It Works

The core pipeline records 2-second audio clips at 48kHz from a USB microphone, resamples them to 16kHz (YAMNet's expected input rate) using SciPy, feeds the waveform into the TensorFlow Lite interpreter, and receives classification scores across all 521 sound categories. If any enabled sound exceeds the 25% confidence threshold, the system logs the detection with a timestamp, emits a WebSocket event for live dashboard updates, and sends a push notification via Pushbullet with a per-sound cooldown to prevent alert spam.

The system includes a priority classification engine that assigns severity levels (1-10) to detected sounds. Critical sounds like fire alarms and glass breaking receive the highest priority and shorter notification cooldowns, while ambient sounds like bird calls or traffic are logged but do not trigger urgent alerts.

Web Dashboard

A Flask-based web dashboard provides real-time monitoring through a dark-themed UI served on the local network. The dashboard shows live detection feeds (updating via WebSocket without page refresh), hourly detection frequency charts, sound category breakdowns, system health indicators (CPU temperature, uptime, memory usage), and controls for starting/stopping monitoring and configuring which sounds to track. Detection data is also logged to timestamped CSV files for later analysis.

Offline Adaptation

A key engineering challenge was adapting the system for fully offline operation. The original design relied on Pushbullet (requiring internet access) for notifications. The offline version replaces this with local push notifications on the Pi itself and stores all alert history locally, making the system viable for field deployment or locations without internet access.

HandWave & Hand Pong

Gesture-Controlled Applications // MediaPipe Hand Landmarker // Real-Time at 30+ FPS

Overview

This project is a suite of gesture-controlled applications powered by Google's MediaPipe Hand Landmarker model, which tracks 21 landmarks on each hand in real time through a standard webcam. What started as a basic experiment with LED feedback evolved into two polished applications: HandWave, a two-hand musical instrument, and Hand Pong, a fully featured Pong game controlled entirely by hand gestures.

HandWave: Gesture-Controlled Instrument

HandWave maps hand position and finger states to real-time audio synthesis. The system tracks one hand and uses finger count to select notes within a musical scale, palm height (Y position) for volume control, palm X position for pitch in theremin mode, and hand roll angle for vibrato depth. A pinch gesture sustains the current note, and swiping left or right cycles through scale modes (pentatonic, major, minor, blues, chromatic).

The application supports three playing modes: Scale mode (finger count picks discrete notes), Theremin mode (continuous pitch mapped to horizontal hand position, like a real theremin), and Chord mode (gestures trigger full chords). Audio is synthesized in real time and can optionally drive physical LEDs through an Arduino serial connection for a visual performance element.

Hand Pong: Gesture-Controlled Game

Hand Pong turns the webcam into a two-player Pong controller. The left hand controls the left paddle and the right hand controls the right paddle, with vertical hand position mapped to paddle movement. Beyond basic controls, the game recognizes five distinct gestures as special abilities:

Pinch = Power Shot (1.8x ball speed, fire trail, screen shake)
Fist = Shield (paddle doubles in size temporarily)
Rock On = Curve Ball (adds spin, ball curves mid-flight)
Thumbs Up = Taunt (cosmetic, displays messages)
Peace Sign = Restart (after game over)
Toggle display modes (webcam vs wireframe-only)

The game features particle explosions on every hit, charge bars for power shot and shield abilities, a rally counter that tracks consecutive hits, screen shake on scoring, random on-screen text reactions, and a countdown timer before each round. The display can toggle between a normal webcam overlay and a clean wireframe mode that shows only the hand skeleton and game elements over a dark background.

EchoShift Expedition

EchoShift Expedition is a 2D puzzle-platformer built in Godot Engine 4.5.1 for the TSA Game Design event. The game's central mechanic is the "EchoBand," a wrist-mounted device that allows the player to record their movements and replay them as a semi-transparent duplicate (an "echo"). This creates cooperative puzzles where the player must work with their own past self: an echo can hold down a pressure plate while the real player passes through a gate, or ride a moving platform to a location the player cannot reach directly.

Beyond basic echo recording, the game introduces three time-manipulation abilities that layer on top of the core mechanic. Rewind reverses an object's state to its previous position. Forward-Shift projects objects into their future state. Stabilize freezes an object in temporal stasis, locking it in place regardless of physics. Level design follows a teach-test-extend philosophy, where each mechanic is introduced in isolation, tested in a focused challenge, then combined with previous mechanics in increasingly complex puzzles.

All pixel art, chiptune music, and sound effects are original creations by the team. The game was built for WebGL browser deployment to meet TSA's playability requirements.

Evolution of Transportation

This project is an interactive, on-rails museum simulation built for the TSA Virtual Reality Simulation event. The experience takes the viewer through eight eras of transportation history, from walking to spaceflight, with each era featuring its own 3D environment, vehicle model, sound design, and narration voice lines.

The simulation was built in Godot 4.5.1 with a Path3D-based ride system that moves the camera along scripted paths through each era's environment. Audio management handles crossfading between ambient sound layers, vehicle sound effects, one-shot events (whistles, horns, rocket launches), and narration. Originally designed for VR headset rendering, the project was adapted to a flatscreen "simulated VR" mode with mouse-look camera controls due to hardware constraints at the competition venue. This required significant rework of the camera positioning, movement systems, and HUD layout.

The eight eras are: Walking, Horseback, Horse-Drawn Carriage, Steamboat, Steam Train, Automobile, Early Airplane, and Spaceship. Each era transition uses a fade-to-black sequence with environment and lighting changes to match the time period.
